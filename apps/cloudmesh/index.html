Weather apps should not cost monthly

The weather app landscape has a problem. The apps that used to be free now charge subscriptions. The ones that are still free are loaded with ads and sell your location data to brokers. And nearly all of them pull from the same handful of data sources, which means the forecast you get from one $4/month app is functionally identical to what you get from another $4/month app.

There is a deeper accuracy issue, too. The nearest official weather station to your house might be 10 or 15 miles away, at an airport or a university campus. When the forecast says it is 72 degrees, that is the temperature at the station, not at your house. Microclimates, elevation differences, urban heat islands, and proximity to water can create significant gaps between what the forecast says and what you actually experience when you walk outside.

Meanwhile, widgets, the primary way most people check weather on their phone, are treated as an afterthought. Most weather apps offer two or three widget options, all of them basically the same rectangle showing temperature and an icon. For something you glance at dozens of times a day, the customization options are remarkably limited.

And then there is the privacy angle. Weather apps are notorious for collecting and selling granular location data. A "free" weather app that pings your location every few minutes is not really free. It is a location tracking service that happens to also show you the temperature.

Widgets, intelligence, and a weather mesh

Cloudmesh Weather starts with what you look at most: widgets. There are 29 of them, covering home screen and lock screen, in every size Apple supports. Detailed forecasts, minimal glanceable designs, radar previews, air quality, UV index, sunrise and sunset, precipitation probability. You can build a home screen that tells you everything you need without ever opening the app.

Inside the app, AI weather summaries replace the wall of numbers with plain-language descriptions of what actually matters. Instead of parsing humidity percentages and dew points, you get something like: "Cool morning, warming to mid-70s by lunch. Rain likely after 4pm, bring a jacket if you're out late." It tells you what to do, not just what the atmosphere is doing.

The most ambitious feature is the community mesh network. Every iPhone has a barometer built into it. Cloudmesh can optionally collect anonymous barometric pressure readings from users' phones and aggregate them into a dense, real-time pressure map. When thousands of phones in a metro area are reporting pressure data, you get hyperlocal weather sensing that no traditional weather station network can match. All readings are anonymous. No location history is stored. No user profiles are built.

Cloudmesh also includes earthquake detection in beta. By monitoring accelerometer data from participating phones, the app can detect seismic waves and notify nearby users. It is an early-stage feature, but the concept is proven: a dense network of sensors can detect earthquakes faster than traditional seismograph networks, potentially providing crucial seconds of warning.

What you get

  • 29 widgets — Home screen and lock screen widgets in every size, from minimal to detailed
  • AI weather summaries — Plain-language forecasts that tell you what you need to know
  • Community mesh network — Anonymous barometric readings from user phones for hyperlocal weather data
  • Earthquake detection (beta) — Accelerometer-based seismic detection with early warning notifications
  • No subscription — One-time purchase. No ads, no data selling, no premium tiers
  • Anonymous by design — No accounts, no location history, no user profiles
