Your cycle data is deeply personal

Period tracking apps handle some of the most intimate health data on your phone. Cycle dates, symptoms, sexual activity, fertility indicators, mood patterns. This is information most people would not share with their closest friends, let alone a corporation's database.

After the Dobbs decision overturned Roe v. Wade in 2022, millions of women in the United States deleted their period trackers. The fear was straightforward: if cycle data is stored on a company's servers, it can be subpoenaed. Prosecutors in states with abortion restrictions could potentially use missed period data, late cycles, or fertility indicators as evidence. This was not a theoretical concern. Law enforcement had already used digital evidence from search histories and text messages in reproductive rights cases.

The response from most tracking apps was reassuring language. "We don't sell your data." "We take your privacy seriously." "We would fight any subpoena." But these statements miss the fundamental problem. If a company has your data on their servers, they can be compelled to hand it over regardless of their privacy policy. The only data that cannot be subpoenaed from a company is data that company never had in the first place.

Even outside the legal context, the privacy picture is bleak. Popular period trackers have been caught sharing data with Facebook, selling anonymized data to third parties, and using tracking pixels that connect your cycle data to your advertising profile. "Free" period tracking has a cost, and you are paying with the most personal health information you have.

Cycle tracking that never leaves your device

Cyla was designed from the ground up with a simple principle: your cycle data should exist in exactly one place, and that place is your phone.

There are no accounts to create. No email addresses to enter. No cloud sync. No servers. When you log a period, a symptom, or a note, that data is written to your device's local storage, encrypted by iOS, and stays there. Cyla does not have servers to store your data on, which means there is nothing to subpoena, nothing to breach, and nothing to sell.

Cycle predictions are calculated on-device using your logged history. If you wear an Apple Watch, Cyla can read wrist temperature data to help confirm ovulation and improve prediction accuracy. This integration uses Apple's HealthKit framework, which means the temperature data stays within Apple's encrypted health ecosystem and is never transmitted to us.

If you ever want to leave, you can export all of your data as a JSON file. It is your data. You should be able to take it with you.

What you get

  • On-device predictions — Cycle and fertility predictions calculated locally on your phone
  • Apple Watch temperature — Wrist temperature integration to help confirm ovulation timing
  • No account required — No email, no sign-up, no cloud. Open the app and start tracking
  • Local encryption — All data encrypted by iOS on your device's secure enclave
  • Full data export — Export everything as JSON anytime you want
  • One-time purchase — Pay once, own it forever. No subscriptions
