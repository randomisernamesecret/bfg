Making Maths Stick

Most maths apps for children fall into one of two traps. The first is over-gamification: the child spends twenty minutes popping balloons, collecting coins, and watching animations, but only answers a handful of actual maths questions along the way. The game is fun, but the learning is incidental. The second trap is the opposite — digitised worksheets. Rows of sums on a screen, no feedback, no adaptation. It is a PDF with a submit button.

Neither approach addresses the real challenge parents face: finding practice that matches what their child is actually learning at school. A Year 3 child working on column addition does not need algebra puzzles or counting games. They need column addition questions at the right difficulty — not so easy that they coast, not so hard that they shut down.

Then there is the question of retention. Children can pass a topic test on Friday and forget half of it by Monday. This is not a failing — it is how memory works. Without systematic review, short-term knowledge never becomes long-term fluency. Most apps treat topics as linear: finish one, move to the next, never look back. The result is children who seem to understand in the moment but cannot recall when it matters — like the Year 4 Multiplication Tables Check or KS2 SATs.

How Arithmetix Addresses This

Arithmetix is built around the UK National Curriculum from Reception through Year 6. Topics are organised by year group and strand — number and place value, addition and subtraction, multiplication and division, fractions, measurement, geometry, and statistics. When you select your child's year group, they get questions that match what they should be learning in class, not random content from across the entire primary curriculum.

The difficulty adapts based on accuracy. If your child gets eight out of ten correct on a topic, the next set gets slightly harder. If they drop below six out of ten, the difficulty eases. This keeps them in what educational researchers call the "zone of proximal development" — challenged enough to learn, supported enough not to give up.

Spaced repetition handles the forgetting problem. When your child demonstrates accuracy on a topic, Arithmetix schedules it for review — first after a day, then three days, then a week, then longer intervals. Topics where they made mistakes come back sooner. This is the same technique used in language learning apps and medical school flashcards, applied to primary maths. Weak areas resurface automatically, without you having to manage the schedule.

Times tables from 2 to 12 are given dedicated practice modes, because fluent recall of multiplication facts underpins almost everything else in KS2 maths. The Year 4 Multiplication Tables Check specifically tests this, and children who are not fluent in their tables struggle not just on the MTC itself but on fractions, division, and multi-step problems throughout Years 5 and 6.

A virtual pet keeps motivation consistent. It responds to daily practice rather than perfect scores, so children who find maths difficult are not punished for getting questions wrong. The goal is to encourage showing up regularly, which matters more for long-term progress than occasional intense cramming sessions.
